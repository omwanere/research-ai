SUMMARY
15
District heating networks (DHNs) have significant potential to decarbonize residential heating
16
and accelerate the energy transition. However, designing carbon-neutral DHNs requires bal-
17
ancing several objectives, including economic costs, social acceptance, long-term uncertain-
18
ties, and grid-integration challenges from electrification. By combining modeling-to-generate-
19
alternatives with power flow simulation techniques, we develop a decision-support method for
20
designing carbon-neutral DHNs that are cost-effective, socially acceptable, robust to future risks,
21
and impose minimal impacts on the electricity grid. Applying our method to a Dutch case, we find
22
substantial diversity in how carbon-neutral DHNs can be designed. The flexibility in technology
23
choice, sizing, and location enables accommodating different real-world needs and achieving
24
high electrification levels without increasing grid loading. For instance, intelligently located heat
25
pumps and thermal storage can limit grid stress even when renewable baseload heat sources
26
and green-fuel boilers are scarce. Using our method, planners can explore diverse carbon-
27
neutral DHN designs and identify the design that best balances stakeholders’ preferences.

INTRODUCTION
29
Heating is the largest energy end-use globally, accounting for half of total energy consumption1,2.
30
Most of the heat generated is used in the residential sector—second only to industry—to meet
31
space heating and domestic hot water needs in buildings3,4. In the European Union (EU), resi-
32
dential heating is responsible for 40% of total energy consumption, with roughly two-thirds of the
33
heat supply still based on fossil fuels5,6. The reliance on fossil fuel for residential heating is even
34
more pronounced in some individual EU Member States; for example, in the Netherlands, around
35
85% of residential heating is supplied by natural gas7,8. Consequently, the decarbonization of
36
residential heating has become a central pillar for accelerating the energy transition globally9–11,
37
and particularly in the EU12–14. Besides climate goals, decarbonizing residential heating will also
38
reduce the EU’s reliance on imported fossil fuels and increase its energy security in an era of
39
fading geopolitical stability15,16.
40
There are several potential pathways for decarbonizing residential heat supply17–21. How-
41
ever, two main pathways have received particular attention in policy debates and academia:
42
decentralized heating using individual building-level heat pumps22–24, and centralized heating
43
1
arXiv:2603.12202v1  [eess.SY]  12 Mar 2026
through carbon-neutral district heating networks (DHNs)25–27. While decentralized heating using
44
individual heat pumps has significant potential to decarbonize residential heat supply28, large-
45
scale deployment faces several practical limitations, such as substantial space requirements
46
and the need for higher building insulation29,30. This poses a significant challenge in the EU,
47
where most of the residential building stock is old, poorly insulated, and difficult to retrofit at
48
scale6. Centralized heating through carbon-neutral DHNs offers an alternative or complemen-
49
tary decarbonization pathway, as it can meet user preferences across buildings with different
50
characteristics25,27. Moreover, DHNs enable the integration of diverse heat sources and thermal
51
energy storage, which increases system flexibility and resilience31. The deployment of DHNs is
52
particularly appealing in the EU since approximately 70% of buildings are located in urban areas
53
with high heat demand density, making district heating more cost-efficient than decentralized
54
heat pumps32–34. As highlighted in the Heat Roadmap Europe reports, district heating will play a
55
key role in the transition to a sustainable, resilient, and affordable energy system in Europe, with
56
its market share in the heat supply mix set to expand from 13% today to 55% by 205014,34.
57
Given its key role in the heat transition, many studies have investigated different strategies
58
to design cost-effective DHNs that, unlike traditional DHNs relying on fossil-fuel combustion,
59
use carbon-neutral heat sources. Among these strategies are power-to-heat (P2H) technologies
60
such as large-scale centralized heat pumps and electric boilers that rely on the—increasingly
61
carbon-neutral—electricity system for heat production, as well as geothermal and other low-
62
temperature heat sources that require electricity to upgrade heat supply temperatures. Con-
63
sequently, the decarbonization of DHNs will further accentuate the interactions between heat
64
and electricity systems, making their operation and planning increasingly interdependent35,36.
65
This growing interdependence has increased the need for integrated heat–electricity system
66
planning37. Prior research on the decarbonization of DHNs has ignored these interactions38–45,
67
or mostly focused on electricity generation capacity adequacy challenges, to assess the addi-
68
tional generation capacity required to support the decarbonization of DHNs17,46,47. These stud-
69
ies therefore provide limited insight into electricity network bottlenecks, as they typically rely on
70
highly simplified models that lack spatial resolution.
71
The electricity network is increasingly recognized as a critical enabler or bottleneck of the en-
72
ergy transition48,49, and must be modeled with high spatial resolution to determine where and to
73
what extent network upgrades are needed to support decarbonization. In an attempt to address
74
this, recent studies used integrated optimization frameworks to jointly optimize the planning of
75
DHNs and electricity networks19,20,50,51. However, these studies have two major limitations. First,
76
although spatially resolved, they typically rely on a simplified representation of the electricity net-
77
work due to computational limitations in including detailed power flow equations in large-scale
78
optimization models. As a result, such models are inadequate for identifying operational bottle-
79
necks, such as transformer and line loadings, which are crucial for the management and planning
80
of electricity network infrastructure52,53. Second, integrated optimization assumes common own-
81
ership, which is intrinsically misaligned with real-world settings since these networks are typically
82
owned and operated by different stakeholders. Hence, integrated optimization may result in im-
83
practical and misleading conclusions54. In the literature, model coupling has been proposed as
84
a technique to address these limitations of integrated optimization55. Model coupling links dif-
85
ferent computational models through interface variables, enabling high-resolution analysis with
86
high-fidelity models while respecting system boundaries36,55. Despite its advantages, the ap-
87
plication of model coupling to investigate the electricity network implications of decarbonizing
88
DHNs is lacking in the literature. Therefore, a high-resolution assessment of electricity network
89
operational bottlenecks arising from the decarbonization of DHNs—particularly when accounting
90
for more realistic planning practices—remains largely unexplored.
91
Another gap persists in the literature: existing studies strive to identify the “least-cost” technol-
92
ogy configuration—under a handful of narrative scenarios—required to decarbonize DHNs42,46,51.
93
2
However, not looking beyond the “least-cost” solution can be short-sighted due to the inability
94
of cost-optimization models to capture harder-to-model dimensions such as structural uncer-
95
tainty, social acceptance, system resilience, and other political aspects56–58. This is particularly
96
relevant in the context of decarbonizing DHNs as it requires long-term investment decisions un-
97
der deep uncertainty, and involves multiple stakeholders with potentially conflicting objectives.
98
For instance, while residual heat from industries is a cost-effective, low-carbon heat source, its
99
long-term availability is uncertain as industries decarbonize or may relocate59. Public opposition
100
may limit the deployment of geothermal heat sources60, while space limitations may restrict the
101
build-out of pipeline infrastructure needed to connect remote heat sources to demand61. These
102
multi-dimensional uncertainties are difficult to parameterize in a model, making the insistence on
103
a single “least-cost” solution of limited practical use to real-world stakeholders62,63.
104
Moreover, studies that focus solely on the “least-cost” system design provide limited gener-
105
alizable insights for accelerating the decarbonization of DHNs in other regions. This is because
106
DHNs are inherently local systems, and technology choices are strongly shaped by site-specific
107
or local conditions. For instance, the uneven distribution of geothermal resources across Eu-
108
rope14 implies that geothermal heat might be technically infeasible in some regions, despite
109
being a cost-effective heat source. The potential for exploiting residual heat also varies across
110
regions, as its feasibility depends on proximity to demand centers and the temporal alignment
111
between industrial activities and local heat demand. Regions with a highly congested electricity
112
network may experience a significant slowdown in the uptake of P2H technologies, regardless
113
of their economic attractiveness. All these factors play out at a more local scale and define what
114
is technically and socially feasible before cost considerations. Therefore, the lack of analysis
115
beyond purely economic assessment leaves planners with a limited understanding of how dis-
116
parities in local conditions reshape DHN design, specifically regarding the trade-offs that arise
117
when the deployment of specific technologies is constrained by local conditions.
118
This paper addresses the above gaps by conducting the first high-resolution assessment of a
119
broad range of technology configurations for decarbonizing DHNs and their potential impacts on
120
the electricity network. Specifically, we address the following questions: (i) What is the range of
121
alternative, economically-comparable technology configurations for decarbonizing DHNs, and to
122
what extent do these configurations differ in their electricity network impacts? (ii) What trade-offs
123
are involved when the deployment of certain low-carbon heat supply technologies is constrained
124
by local conditions, and what are the spillover effects on the electricity network?
125
In doing so, we develop a model coupling framework that links a capacity-expansion and
126
operation-optimization model of a DHN to an AC power flow simulation model of an electric-
127
ity network. Model coupling enables a high-resolution assessment of temporally and spatially
128
resolved electricity network bottlenecks that are difficult to capture with monolithic integrated
129
optimization frameworks. Also, model coupling more closely reflects real-world practices as it
130
retains individual ownership of each network model. Our approach follows a sequential work-
131
flow (see section Methodology framework in experimental procedures) in which we first apply
132
the Modeling to Generate Alternatives (MGA) technique to the DHN model to generate many
133
near-optimal carbon-neutral DHN designs. MGA allows us to explicitly account for structural un-
134
certainty by systematically exploring a broad option space of similarly costly but technologically
135
diverse carbon-neutral DHN designs. By moving beyond prescribing a single “least-cost” design
136
to uncovering an option space of alternatives, we reveal the flexibility in choice, location, and
137
sizing of heat supply technologies, thermal storage, and heat pipeline infrastructure required to
138
achieve carbon neutrality. This option space can help planners understand the trade-offs re-
139
quired to accommodate harder-to-model dimensions—such as social acceptance and long-term
140
uncertainty in resource availability—and to balance potentially conflicting preferences across
141
different stakeholders.
142
Subsequently, we quantify the electricity network impacts of each carbon-neutral DHN design
143
3
by using its spatially resolved electricity profiles as inputs to the AC power flow simulation. We
144
synthesize the outputs from both models into an integrated heat-electricity network planning
145
decision space that maps DHN design characteristics to their corresponding electricity network
146
loadings. Network operators can explore this decision space to examine cross-network trade-
147
offs and support coordinated network expansion planning. In sum, using our method, energy
148
system planners can identify economically comparable DHN designs that are more robust to
149
future risks and impose minimal impacts on the electricity network.
150
To showcase the potential of our method, we apply it to a case study in the Dutch region
151
of South Holland. We focus on one of its largest existing DHNs (see Figure S1), which is pro-
152
jected to expand substantially in the future, reaching an annual heat demand of about 2700
153
GWh and a peak capacity of 1400 MW by 2050 (see Figure S3). For the electricity system, we
154
model the current regional network (see Figure S2). In addition to the spatial and temporally
155
resolved electricity profiles from the DHN model, we also include profiles from other consumers
156
connected to the same network. This allows us to assess the overall grid impact induced by the
157
decarbonization of the DHN in the context of broader regional electrification. As a starting point,
158
we apply MGA to generate 515 near-optimal solutions that represent alternative DHN designs
159
that are maximally different from the least-cost solution while total system cost remains within
160
10% of the least-cost case. Additionally, we examine the effects of tightening and further relax-
161
ing this cost budget as a proxy for technology cost uncertainty, and also investigate scenarios
162
with different weather years and demand projections (see section Sensitivity analysis results in
163
supplemental information). This yields a total of 3,605 alternative carbon-neutral DHN designs,
164
each of which is subject to a full-year, hourly-resolution AC power flow simulation to quantify
165
their corresponding electricity network impacts using transformer and line loadings. Given the
166
extent of our analysis, we cannot examine all results with the same degree of detail here. How-
167
ever, we release all our results freely on Zenodo64, and also make our code open source65 for
168
transparency and reproducibility.
169
Our contribution extends beyond the methodology as we also advance the broader discussion
170
on planning future carbon-neutral DHNs. First, our systematic exploration of the near-optimal
171
design space captures the interactions between technology options and reveals untold design
172
features and associated trade-offs. For instance, we show the existence of diverse strategies
173
to achieve higher DHN electrification levels without increasing electricity network loading—such
174
as spatially distributing the deployment of P2H units but at the expense of more heat pipeline
175
infrastructure build-out, or by prioritizing heat pumps over electric boilers, with additional thermal
176
storage to limit heat pipeline expansion. Second, by considering many technologies and explicitly
177
analyzing cases in which certain options are unavailable, we make our findings generalizable by
178
capturing trade-offs that could appear in any region, showing how location-specific constraints
179
shape the design space. Finally, our extensive discussion provides insights into the potential role
180
of different technologies in future carbon-neutral DHNs, particularly when interactions with the
181
electricity network and resource constraints are considered.
182
We approach the presentation of our results in four steps. We first discuss the model results
183
for the least-cost solution as a basis of comparison. Next, we present a broader option space of
184
near-optimal carbon-neutral DHN designs to reveal the trade-offs it would entail to accommodate
185
certain design preferences. We then assess these alternative DHN designs within an integrated
186
heat-electricity network planning decision space to quantify their electricity network impacts.
187
Finally, we investigate how local technology availability or deployment constraints reshape the
188
feasible design space and associated electricity network impact outcomes.

RESULTS
190
Cost-minimization concentrates investment in technology con-
191
figurations with high deployment risks and electricity network
192
loading
193
The model results for the conventional cost-minimization run are shown in Figure 1. The least-
194
cost system design for decarbonizing the DHN suggests a high investment in electric boilers and
195
green gas boilers, each reaching roughly 400 MW of installed capacity. This is complemented
196
by 200 MW of industrial residual heat and 185 MW of geothermal capacity, while heat pumps
197
and hydrogen boilers play only a marginal role (also see Figure S11), with a combined capacity
198
of about 25 MW. The system further relies on short-term thermal storage in steel water tanks,
199
providing 320 MW of discharge thermal power and 1920 MWh of energy buffer, together with a
200
moderate build-out of heat pipeline infrastructure of around 415 MW.
201
Although cost-optimal and technically feasible, several features of the least-cost solution ap-
202
pear potentially undesirable when viewed through a broader planning lens. First, the least-cost
203
design relies heavily on green gas—which is biogas upgraded to the Dutch natural gas quality
204
standards such that it can be directly injected into the current natural gas grid66. Energy transition
205
scenarios in Europe anticipate strong cross-sectoral competition for biomass67, which is needed
206
for green gas production. As biomass is both resource-limited and contested68, uncertainty re-
207
mains regarding the amount of green gas that will be allocated for residential heating69. There-
208
fore, while cost-effective, large green gas capacity build-out could result in underutilized capacity
209
or even undermine system resilience if green gas availability becomes more constrained or ex-
210
pensive than projected. Second, more than half of the baseload capacity is provided by industrial
211
residual heat from a nearby petrochemical industry in Rotterdam. However, as for many simi-
212
lar industrial residual heat projects globally59,70,71, the long-term availability of this heat source
213
is also uncertain, given societal opposition to fossil-fuel industries and the potential decline of
214
refinery or similar heavy-industry activities as economies decarbonize. Third, the least-cost so-
215
lution entails a substantial deployment of geothermal, which aligns with the real-world ambition
216
to develop four geothermal wells in The Hague (see Figure S10). Still, concentrating geothermal
217
infrastructure in a single urban area may face practical barriers, including social acceptance and
218
permitting challenges60,72.
219
Finally, another potentially undesirable feature is the higher deployment of electric boilers
220
instead of more efficient heat pumps.
This is because electric boilers are about five times
221
cheaper than heat pumps in investment cost (see Table S1), and therefore—despite the lower ef-
222
ficiency—are an economically attractive heat electrification option for DHN operators. However,
223
a high reliance on less efficient electric boilers imposes severe stress on the electricity grid, as
224
shown by the transformer and line loading duration curves in Figure 1. Moreover, to minimize
225
heat network expansion costs, the cost-optimal deployment of electric boilers is near demand
226
centers, further increasing loading in already congested transformers and lines while those with
227
more available capacity remain underutilized (see Figures S12 and S13 for the loading duration
228
curves of all transformers and lines in the electricity network). From an integrated system per-
229
spective, the implication is a shift in costs from the DHN to the electricity grid since accommodat-
230
ing the resulting peak electricity flows would require substantial investment in grid reinforcement.
231
These outcomes are an artifact of cost-minimization, which hinges solely on economic perfor-
232
mance to identify the “least-cost” system design. Therefore, solely relying on cost-minimization
233
to design carbon-neutral DHNs may result in technology pathways that are more vulnerable to
234
future risks and uncertainties, and that impose substantial stress on the electricity grid.

A broad range of diverse district heating system designs ex-
249
ists near the cost-optimum
250
Given that some features of the least-cost DHN design may be undesirable in practice and
251
therefore warrant modification, we want to explore economically comparable design alternatives
252
and investigate whether and to what extent these undesirable features can be mitigated, and
253
the trade-offs that this would entail. To do so, we implement an upgraded version of the MGA
254
SPORES algorithm (see section Generating SPORES in experimental procedures) to generate
255
515 alternative carbon-neutral DHN system designs. These alternatives, which we call SPORES,
256
represent feasible system designs, namely configurations of technology deployment, that are
257
maximally different from the least-cost solution while total system cost remains within 10% of the
258
least-cost case.
259
Figure 2(A) illustrates the frequency distribution of potential capacity utilization across the
260
near-optimal decision space. We see that, apart from pipelines, which are required to distribute
261
heat throughout the network, all technologies can attain zero utilization of their potential capacity.
262
This implies that apart from heat pipelines, all technologies are “real choices”; that is, they are not
263
a strict “must-have” since they can be fully substituted by a combination of functionally equivalent
264
alternatives, irrespective of the accepted cost relaxation (see Figures S23 and S24). However,
265
such substitutions entail significant trade-offs, revealing key interactions between technology
266
choices.
267
To illustrate these trade-offs, we highlight a few SPORES in Figure 2(A), and also show their
268
technology configurations in Figure 2(B). We can see, for instance, that: (i) reducing reliance
269
on green gas boilers (see low-boiler greengas SPORE) from 400 MW in the least-cost solu-
270
tion to 100 MW is possible by increasing the deployment of hydrogen (+240 MW) and electric
271
(+100 MW) boilers. This additional peaking capacity reduces the need for storage (-25 MW).
272
At the same time, geothermal capacity may increase slightly (+15 MW) to compensate for the
273
decrease in heat pumps (-15 MW). (ii) Decreasing electric boiler capacity (see low-electric
274
boiler & geothermal SPORE) from 400 MW to 70 MW is possible by deploying higher heat
275
pump (+160 MW) and hydrogen boiler (+ 200 MW) capacity, and consequently, higher storage
276
capacity (+130 MW) to exploit low price periods in electricity and hydrogen, which are more
277
volatile than green gas (see Figures S4, S5, and S6). This also increases system flexibility
278
and reduces the need for baseload technologies such as geothermal (-45 MW). (iii) Reducing
279
residual heat (see low-residual heat SPORE) from 200 MW to 90 MW, which provides cheap
280
7
baseload capacity, can be compensated by higher deployment of waste-to-energy (+200 MW)
281
and geothermal (+5 MW), coupled with higher heat pipeline expansion (+255 MW) to connect
282
these remote heat sources to demand centers.
283
Interestingly, we find that technologies such as CHPs, seasonal storage (HT-ATES), and solar
284
thermal play only a limited role and are therefore, not crucial to the system as their utilization
285
remains relatively low across all SPORES, irrespective of the cost relaxation (see Figures S23
286
and S24), weather year (see Figures S25 and S26), or heat demand scenario (see Figures S27
287
and S28). For CHPs and HT-ATES, their limited utilization is primarily due to their high investment
288
cost. For solar thermal, although its maximum potential capacity can be fully utilized even under
289
small cost relaxations (see Figure S23), its utilization frequency remains consistently low across
290
most SPORES. This is because of the structural mismatch between its seasonal production and
291
heat demand that requires substantial additional investment in storage and pipeline capacity
292
(see Figures S15 and S16), making it less competitive than other alternatives.
293
Assessing the effects of increasing the utilization of CHPs, HT-ATES, and solar thermal—by
294
explicitly maximizing their deployment (see section Generating SPORES in experimental proce-
295
dures)—shows that under a higher cost budget, additional capacity in these technologies simply
296
co-exists with the current least-cost deployment rates of other technologies (see Figures S15
297
and S16). In other words, increasing the capacity of these technologies does not trigger sig-
298
nificant substitutions but instead represents a parallel, higher-cost pathway. Further trade-offs
299
associated with increasing other preferences can be explored using the full dataset of our results
300
freely available on Zenodo64. Overall, we find that a marginal increase in cost creates substan-
301
tial flexibility in how a carbon-neutral DHN can be designed (also see Figures S23 and S24).
302
This flexibility in technology choice, sizing, and location provides maneuvering space to mitigate
303
the potentially undesirable features of the least-cost solution, and to accommodate other un-
304
modeled preferences or real-world needs, such as enhanced system resilience through greater
305
technology and supply diversity.

Higher levels of electrification can be achieved without increas- 318
ing electricity network loading
319
We have shown in the previous section that maneuvering space exists to reduce or completely
320
eliminate reliance on technologies associated with potentially undesirable features of the least-
321
cost solution. So far, we assessed the trade-offs within this maneuvering space only from the
322
DHN perspective. We now proceed to examine these trade-offs from an integrated heat–electricity
323
system perspective to (i) determine the extent to which alternative DHN designs differ in their
324
electricity network loading and (ii) identify key design features that impose impact loading on the
325
electricity network. To achieve this, we subject each of SPORE to a full AC power flow simulation
326
to quantify its impact on the electricity network, reflected by transformer and line loadings. We
327
then synthesize the results from both the heat and electricity model runs for each SPORE into
328
an integrated decision space to quantify cross-network trade-offs using the heat and electricity
329
network assessment metrics defined in Table 1.
330
Examining the impact of each SPORE or near-optimal DHN design on the electricity network
331
shows that, overall, SPORES exhibit significant variations in grid loadings (see Figures S19
332
and S20). The integrated decision space, which maps the characteristics of SPORES to their
333
corresponding electricity network loadings, is shown in Figure 3. We highlight the subset of
334
generated SPORES with lower electricity network loading compared to the least-cost solution
335
across all electricity network metrics.
That is, these SPORES fall within all lower electricity
336
network loading envelopes represented by the dashed rectangles. We further highlight selected
337
SPORES in Figure 3(A)to illustrate the underlying cross-network trade-offs, and zero in on these
338

352

353

354

355

356

357

358

For instance, the high-electrification SPORE deploys 20 %
359

360

361

362

363

364

365

366

367

368

369

Location-specific constraints transform real choices into prac-
408
tical must-haves
409
The maneuvering space described in section A broad range of diverse district heating system
410
designs exists near the cost-optimum represents the purely techno-economic flexibility available
411
to system planners to accommodate other practically desirable features or unmodeled prefer-
412
ences, assuming all the considered technology options are available. However, as previously
413
mentioned, DHNs are inherently local, and therefore, the concrete technology configurations
414
depend on local constraints. We now want to re-examine this option space to see how plausi-
415
ble local constraints may impact the trade-offs that we have revealed so far, thereby providing
416
insights that may be generalizable to a broader range of DHNs. To do so, we consider the follow-
417
ing set of plausible local constraints: (i) unfavorable subsurface conditions for the deployment of
418
geothermal and HT-ATES; (ii) insufficient proximity to residual heat sources; (iii) restricted space
419
availability for large solar thermal installations; (iv) limited availability or lack of existence of a
420
reliable supply chain for less established technologies such as green gas and hydrogen boilers;
421
(v) limited heat electrification in the case of a congested electricity network. We then filter the
422
previously generated SPORES to retain only those that satisfy these constraints.
423
We see (Figure 4) that, unsurprisingly, the potential unavailability of technology options that
424
already had a low deployment frequency in the unconstrained option space, such as solar ther-
425
mal and HT-ATES, does not dramatically affect the maneuvering space for designing the carbon-
426
neutral DHN. Moreover, when explicitly considering the subset of SPORES that ensure lim-
427
ited grid impact, the changes are identical to those observed in the initial unconstrained option
428
space (see Figure S21), most notably, a reduced reliance on electric boilers. Other design deci-
429
sions, such as whether to rely more on gas boilers, non-dispatchable (baseload) sources, or heat
430
pumps, remain “real choices”. Contrarily, changes to the design space are more pronounced,
431
instead, when the reliance on either green gas or hydrogen boilers is constrained by local so-
432
cial preferences or supply chain scarcity (also see Figure S22). When both such boilers are,
433
for instance, limited to a capacity of no more than 20% of peak demand, ensuring the economic
434
competitiveness of the DHN requires limiting reliance on less cost-effective technologies, such as
435
CHPs, solar thermal, HT-ATES, waste-to-energy, and—though to a lesser extent—also baseload
436
sources, pipeline, and very large heat pump capacities. If mitigating grid impact is factored
437
15
in, the design options to compensate for limited gas boiler capacity reduce further, with elec-
438
tric boilers—an obvious functionally equivalent substitute—almost ceasing to be a viable option.
439
The consequence is a need to maximize the limited available capacity of cost-effective green
440
gas boilers alongside as-high-as-possible (subject to budget constraints) deployment of efficient
441
heat pumps and conventional TES far beyond least-cost levels (see Figures S35 to S40 in Sen-
442
sitivity analysis results). However, constraining electric boilers does not nearly have the same
443
effect; limiting their deployment mainly affects budget considerations as it marginally shrinks the
444
overall maneuvering space, but substantially benefits grid-related considerations, irrespective of
445
the scenario (see Figures S35 to S40), consistent with the findings in section Higher levels of
446
electrification can be achieved without increasing electricity network loading.
447
The most challenging limitation is dealing with the lack of baseload heat sources, such as
448
geothermal and residual heat (also see Figures S35 to S40). In line with the trade-offs we high-
449
lighted in prior sections, Figure 4 shows that the complete absence of such sources still leaves
450
several options for designing a cost-effective carbon-neutral DHN. However, factoring in the si-
451
multaneous need to mitigate grid impacts substantially reduces the maneuvering space for other
452
decisions. If geothermal is not an option locally, then trading off such a heat source with moder-
453
ate reliance on residual heat partly compensates for it. However, a simultaneous higher reliance
454
on gas boilers is necessary to keep both costs and grid impact limited (also see Figures S35
455
to S40). Meanwhile, higher heat pump capacities, alongside conventional thermal storage, must
456
be prioritized, too, over economically convenient but less efficient electric boilers, when minimiz-
457
ing grid impact becomes a priority. What is more, a careful, intelligent spatial deployment of such
458
P2H assets is critical, in line with our findings in section Higher levels of electrification can be
459
achieved without increasing electricity network loading. When residual heat is the unavailable
460
source, our results show a similar, albeit more pronounced, dynamic. This is because residual
461
heat, in our case study (see section Technology modeling in experimental procedures), is already
462
at high temperatures and therefore, unlike geothermal, requires no auxiliary electricity for tem-
463
perature upgrades, making it more challenging to maintain low grid stress within the remaining
464
technology options and budget (also see Figures S35 to S40). This may differ for residual heat
465
sources at lower temperatures. Our case study, therefore, plays a role in the exact magnitude
466
of the remaining design maneuvering space in Figure 4 when neither geothermal nor residual
467
heat is an option. However, the high-level trade-offs we highlight remain valid and generalizable:
468
a lack of baseload heat sources requires greater reliance on carbon-neutral gas boilers and, to
469
the extent allowed by grid constraints, intelligently deployed heat pumps coupled with thermal
470
storage.
471
It is worth noting that simultaneously removing multiple technology options is not always
472
possible. For instance, the lack of baseload heat sources requires some reliance on gas boilers
473
if mitigating grid stress is a concern. Moreover, even when using gas boilers, the simultaneous
474
absence of local geothermal and residual heat does not enable the design of a DHN that meets
475
the grid-integration requirements we have identified. This may, of course, be different for a DHN
476
system whose local electricity grid has greater capacity and can accommodate more electrical
477
assets than the one we used as a case study.

DISCUSSION
491
We have argued that designing a carbon-neutral DHN solely on the basis of minimum cost can
492
lead to several potentially undesirable features, such as over-reliance on heat sources with un-
493
certain availability, concentration of geothermal heat sources in a single location that could stir
494
public opposition, and large electric boiler capacity build-out that could stress the electricity net-
495
work. These features of cost-minimization are not only unique to our case. Broader energy
496
system planning studies have likewise identified similar patterns in least-cost system designs,
497
such as over-deployment of a technology in a single location73, or reliance on a large capacity
498
of a few potentially problematic technologies74 .
499
We concede that the type and severity of potentially undesirable features of a least-cost de-
500
sign may vary across DHNs in different regions. For instance, regions with abundant biomass
501
resources may find it less problematic to deploy large capacities of biomass-based heating tech-
502
nologies. However, for many European countries, such as the Netherlands, which have limited
503
domestic biomass availability68, it remains highly uncertain where sufficient sustainable biomass
504
would be sourced to produce biofuels or green gas at scale66. Therefore, for such countries, de-
505
signing a DHN that relies heavily on biomass-derived fuels represents a technology pathway that
506
is more vulnerable to potential risks from supply uncertainty or import dependence, a concern
507
that has already been raised by real-world stakeholders75.
508
Irrespective of the region, designing a carbon-neutral DHNs requires long-term investment
509
decisions under deep uncertainty, including uncertainties that are difficult to parameterize in a
510
model, such as social acceptance63. Additionally, because electrification will play an important
511
role in the decarbonization of DHNs76, DHN planners must ensure that system designs are not
512
only robust to future uncertainties, but also impose minimal stress on the electricity network since
513
reinforcing the grid is not only costly77, but also slow due to long lead times, permitting delays,
514
material supply constraints, and workforce shortage75.
515
This study, therefore, presents a decision-support method for designing carbon-neutral DHNs
516
that are less vulnerable to future risks and have minimal impact on the grid while remaining near-
517
optimal in cost. By applying MGA within a framework that couples a DHN capacity expansion
518
and operation model with a power flow simulation model of the electricity network, we develop a
519
method to systematically explore similarly costly but technologically diverse DHN designs, and
520
quantify their impacts on the electricity network. Applying our method to a Dutch case study, we
521
demonstrate that with a marginal cost increase above the optimum, there is substantial diversity
522
in how a carbon-neutral DHN can be designed, and the diverse designs exhibit significant varia-
523
tions in grid impact. Within the near-optimal decision space, almost all technologies can be fully
524
substituted by functionally equivalent alternatives, and thus are “real choices”. This flexibility in
525
18
choice, sizing, and location of technologies—while system cost remains near optimal—provides
526
planners with substantial maneuvering space to mitigate potential risks and balance conflicting
527
preferences across different stakeholders in order to reach a consensus solution.
528
Naturally, accommodating certain preferences requires significant trade-offs. For instance,
529
we find that decreasing the deployment of electric boilers—to minimize grid impact—and re-
530
ducing the concentration of geothermal heat sources in The Hague—to mitigate potential risks
531
from public opposition—requires higher deployment of heat pumps and more thermal storage.
532
Similarly, reducing reliance on residual heat from a nearby petrochemical industry can be com-
533
pensated for by higher deployment of waste-to-energy heat sources and more pipeline expan-
534
sion. Although the exact magnitude of these trade-offs will vary across DHNs and regions, the
535
structural dynamics in technology interaction and substitution we uncover here are likely to be
536
consistent in other DHNs. For instance, we find that designs with lower green gas boiler deploy-
537
ment require an almost proportional increase in peaking technologies, such as electric and/or
538
hydrogen boilers, or more baseload heat sources, coupled with higher storage levels. Similar
539
interaction patterns between heating technologies have been observed in a study examining
540
existing DHN technology mixes across different European countries78. Our analysis, however,
541
reveals these dynamics systematically across the full design option space, enabling planners to
542
understand the underlying substitution patterns and quantify the trade-offs required to accom-
543
modate diverse preferences and constraints that match the needs of different stakeholders.
544
The trade-offs associated with reducing reliance on specific technologies become more pro-
545
nounced when we wish to keep grid impact minimal. For instance, under a 10% cost budget
546
above the least-cost case, green gas boilers cannot be reduced below 20% of their potential
547
capacity, while the deployment of electric boilers must be lower than the capacity suggested
548
by the least-cost design across all investigated scenarios if minimizing grid impact is a priority.
549
Using our integrated decision space that maps DHN characteristics to their corresponding grid
550
loading, it is possible to identify DHN designs with low grid impacts. While it is straightforward
551
that such designs will generally have lower levels of electrification, we find that higher levels of
552
electrification can, in fact, be achieved with even lower grid loading. The high-electrification and
553
low-grid loading designs generally (i) have a more intelligent spatial distribution of P2H tech-
554
nologies across the network, thereby reducing concentration in already congested locations but
555
at the expense of more pipeline expansion; (ii) prioritize more efficient heat pumps—combined
556
with higher thermal storage deployment—over electric boilers. These findings refine prevailing
557
narratives in the literature that associate high heat electrification rates with grid stress22,24. Our
558
results show that grid overloads depend not only on the degree of electrification but also on the
559
interaction between technology choices and their spatial deployment patterns.
560
Another, perhaps counterintuitive finding enabled by our model coupling approach is the inter-
561
action between heat electrification and distributed PV generators. We find that some moderately
562
electrified DHN designs reduce transformer overloads compared to low-electrification designs,
563
since P2H units avoid reverse peak power flows upstream by absorbing surplus generation from
564
local PVs. This suggests that heat electrification may alleviate grid congestion resulting from
565
peak supply, and also increase the efficiency of PVs in energy systems with high shares of
566
distributed PV generation. However, the temporal mismatch between heat demand for space
567
heating and PV generation limits the scale of this solution. Perhaps, this could scale up if heat
568
electrification is coupled with higher (seasonal) thermal storage, particularly for designs dom-
569
inated by heat pumps, since heat pumps are most efficient when it is sunnier. A few studies
570
claim that coordinating the operation of PV, heat pump, and thermal storage can accelerate
571
decarbonization35,79. However, more work is needed to determine whether relying on heat elec-
572
trification and thermal storage to absorb excess generation from PVs is economically beneficial
573
from a broader energy system perspective, compared to direct curtailment.
574
A particularly noteworthy discussion concerns hydrogen boilers, whose potential use for res-
575
19
idential heating is highly debated in the literature66,80. Most studies conclude that hydrogen is
576
unlikely to play a role in future DHNs for several reasons, including costs, efficiency losses, and
577
competition with other difficult-to-electrify sectors such as industry and transport81,82. While our
578
least-cost solution indeed aligns with this claim, the near-optimal option space, however, shows
579
that hydrogen boilers emerge as a valuable alternative when reliance on biomass-derived fuels
580
is reduced, and even more so when avoiding electricity network overloads becomes a priority.
581
This implies that for DHNs in regions with limited biomass availability and a highly congested
582
grid, hydrogen boilers could be a potential backup option for security of supply. Therefore, it
583
may be premature to categorically exclude hydrogen boilers from future DHN planning. Instead,
584
their potential role should be assessed in light of regional resource constraints, grid conditions,
585
system resilience, and long-term infrastructure development strategies.
586
Another question concerns the future role of sustainable-fuel CHPs in carbon-neutral DHNs.
587
We find that within the near-optimal option space, both the utilization frequency and deployed
588
capacity of CHPs remain low across all designs. This is primarily because CHPs become more
589
capital-intensive under frequent low electricity price periods in a broader decarbonized energy
590
system dominated by wind and solar. Our finding aligns with a recent EU-wide study that sug-
591
gests a declining role for CHPs in future DHNs14. However, some studies emphasize that CHPs
592
may retain a strategic value for power system decarbonization, generation adequacy, and se-
593
curity of supply when peak demand periods coincide with low wind and solar availability17. Our
594
sensitivity analysis using a cold weather year with low solar and wind output—the so-called cold
595
dark doldrums—shows only a marginal increase in CHP deployment across designs, as the few
596
periods of high electricity prices are insufficient to offset investment costs. This suggests that
597
if CHPs are to play a role in power system decarbonization, relying solely on energy-only mar-
598
ket revenues may not provide sufficient incentive; instead, dedicated policy support or capacity
599
remuneration mechanisms might be required to incentivize their deployment.
600
We also observe a similar pattern for seasonal storage (HT-ATES) and solar thermal across
601
all designs. In our case, these technologies are only deployed when additional budget is made
602
available, without any significant technology substitution, suggesting that their deployment mostly
603
represents a parallel, higher-cost alternative design. Similar conclusions have been drawn in
604
other studies, where HT-ATES is found to be too costly and to significantly increase the lev-
605
elized cost of heat83. For solar thermal, although it can reach its maximum potential capacity
606
in some designs, we find that potential capacity utilization frequency remains low—even in rela-
607
tively warmer weather year—primarily due to the additional investments required in storage and
608
complementary supply technologies to compensate for the seasonal mismatch between solar
609
thermal production and heat demand. A similar dynamic has also been highlighted in the lit-
610
erature for DHNs with significant solar thermal capacity, such as the Gram Fjernvarme DHN in
611
Denmark, where achieving higher solar thermal penetration has required large-scale seasonal
612
storage and integration with multiple additional heat supply technologies78. However, further
613
work is needed to assess the economic viability of these technologies within a broader energy
614
system context, particularly for HT-ATES, which some studies have shown to increase energy
615
system efficiency and reduce overall cost84.
616
Nevertheless, as we have argued previously, DHN design decisions should not be based
617
solely on economic cost.
Deploying technologies that appear consistently at lower capacity
618
across designs, such as CHPs, HT-ATES, and solar thermal, could be justified as a strategy to
619
enhance system resilience through greater flexibility and technological diversity. Such technolo-
620
gies may provide alternative supply pathways, reduce dependence on a few dominant resources,
621
and increase the system’s ability to cope with unexpected disruptions in fuel supply, infrastructure
622
constraints, extreme weather events, or unplanned maintenance. Indeed, the economic value of
623
resilience is difficult to quantify, as its benefits typically materialize only after a shock occurs85.
624
Therefore, it is important to provide planners with the full option space for them to deliberate
625
20
on what is most practically viable for society since the contribution of certain technologies may
626
extend beyond short-term cost performance.
627
The inherently local nature of DHNs implies that some of the technologies we investigate here
628
might not be an option in certain locations. For instance, certain regions may lack geothermal
629
heat sources due to unfavorable geological conditions. To provide more insights for DHNs in
630
other regions, we reexamined the previously described trade-offs under a set of plausible tech-
631
nology availability constraints. We find that a challenging local constraint is the limited availability
632
of fuels, such as hydrogen or biomass, for fuel boilers. When this is the case, prioritizing heat
633
pumps and thermal storage over other technologies is vital to ensure both cost effectiveness
634
and limited grid impact. Even more challenging is the potential local unavailability of geother-
635
mal or residual heat. While this local barrier does not prevent designing a carbon-neutral DHN
636
per se, if minimizing grid impact is a priority, then at least one carbon-neutral baseload heat
637
source (geothermal or residual heat) and a design combining a spatially-optimized large-scale
638
deployment of heat pumps with thermal storage and some form of gas-based peaking technology
639
become indispensable.
640
As with all modeling studies, our work is not without limitations. First, we do not explicitly
641
quantify the extent of grid reinforcement required to accommodate DHN electrification. Grid
642
reinforcement needs depend not only on DHN-related loads but also on other local electricity
643
demands and generation, which are treated as static in our analysis. In practice, some of these
644
static loads will have some flexibility to respond to high network tariffs during potential grid con-
645
gestion periods. Therefore, quantifying grid upgrades without accounting for demand-response
646
and grid tariffs would be misleading. Accordingly, our results should be interpreted as indicating
647
the isolated potential impacts of DHN electrification on the existing grid, rather than as estimates
648
of actual reinforcement requirements.
649
Second, computational limitations shape our modeling choices. The DHN planning model
650
is formulated as a linear program (LP) to avoid the computational complexity associated with
651
non-convex formulations. As a result, hydraulic flow dynamics are not explicitly represented,
652
and we modeled heat flows using a simplified transport model. Moreover, investment decisions
653
allow for fractional technology capacities rather than discrete unit sizes due to the LP formula-
654
tion. Even with this simplification, the large number of MGA runs required to thoroughly explore
655
the near-optimal space was only feasible using high-performance computing resources86. For
656
similar computational reasons, location-specific technology constraints were imposed ex post by
657
filtering the previously computed option space, rather than by re-running the MGA for each new
658
constraint. Endogenously including each constraint and recomputing the near-optimal space
659
would shift the global optimal cost, which is used for setting the cost budget. Our sensitivity anal-
660
ysis on the cost budget can be interpreted as partially capturing the implications of such shifts.
661
A potential solution to improve computational efficiency would be to narrow the search space
662
through stakeholder engagement, for example, via more targeted human-in-the-loop MGA ap-
663
proaches87. All these limitations provide a foundation for future research to focus on.
664
In conclusion, carbon-neutral DHNs are expected to play a key role in decarbonizing residen-
665
tial heating and accelerating the energy transition. Yet, designing carbon-neutral DHNs requires
666
balancing several potentially conflicting objectives, including economic costs, social acceptance,
667
robustness to future risks, and grid integration challenges arising from electrification. By com-
668
bining modeling-to-generate-alternatives with power flow simulation techniques for the first time,
669
this study offers broadly applicable insights as well as a decision-support method for design-
670
ing carbon-neutral DHNs that are cost-effective, socially acceptable, robust to future risks, and
671
impose minimal impacts on the electricity network.
